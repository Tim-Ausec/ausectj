Starting My Career in College

01.06.2025 09:25

My first exploration into web development was through an unpaid virtual internship that I took on for a small laundromat in LA after high school. The laundromat was run by a single guy with dreams of a low-maintanence, passive income stream in the form of this laundromat. He had a few employees, but was generally hell-bent on reducing costs and raising income with as little effort as possible. The point of the internship was to bring in cheap labor to help market the business and increase revenues.

It was very open ended,

But the owner had a few ideas on how we could contribute. One of those ideas was SEO optimization for the laundromat’s website, which was created, hosted, and maintained with WordPress. Apparently, a large percentage of new business was gained through Google’s tools, particularly Google maps. Being the only virtual intern, I figured that I should take a swing at it. So, I watched a lot of videos and took a lot of notes, which turned into configuring a few things in the options of the WordPress interface. I looked into a few more ways to understand the site’s traffic, but eventually realized that I was putting in a lot of work for no pay, so I quit the internship and became a bus boy at a mexican restaurant for the rest of the Summer.

When I got to college in the Fall, I was firehosed with career-related information that my little blue-collar mind was nearly unable to handle. Networking, career fairs, interview tips, professionalism, and of course,

the resume. The resume is not simply the 2 page list of curated accomplishments and activities that you hand your potential employer. While this is the most impactful part of your resume, there are supplemental materials that may help you stand apart from the crowd. The two most fundamental supplemental materials in my field are the LinkedIn profile and the personal website. By the time that I had completed my resume and started to attain a better idea of getting jobs in the professional world, I had become influenced by my roommate about the idea of supplemental materials.

See, my roommate was a CS major.

The thing about CS majors is that they are on a path that sacrifices job security for potentially lucrative positions. Therefore, they must be well-tuned-in on the job acquisition process, or be fed to the wolves. My roommate brought up that it might be a good idea to make a personal website, as it shows employers that you have technical muster and are willing to go the extra mile. I was convinced by this idea after being exposed to a dog-eat-dog mentality for the professional job hunt, and decided to crack open WordPress. After all, I had experience with it! How hard could it be?
